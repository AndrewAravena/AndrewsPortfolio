First Large Scale Project

1/28

In the year 2024, during Year 11 in Technology Product Design , I embarked on my first actual year of self-learning (kind of). My teacher tells the class, "Your project should be flat-packable and fit within the dimensions of an A3 box ." With that, ideas begin to flow; everyone is brainstorming what to create. Then he says we need a stakeholder; we must design this for someone. Ideas grow scarce. Who can I make this for? My mom? My dad? My brother? And what could I create for them? Then an idea pops into my head: a monitor stand. But that's too basic; I need to make it better and more advanced. A monitor stand with an Arduino-powered volume mixer-that's a perfect idea! I need to learn to solder, program, and wire an Arduino Nano. But how will I construct the joints? I don't want to do woodwork; that feels too permanent. I need to make it future-proof. 3D printed joints-now that's an amazing idea! I'll create 3D printed joints that hold the wood together firmly , along with a 3D printed Arduino case to house the wires, switches, and analog dials. I'm using advanced techniques: 3D printing , microcontrollers, and programming. This project turned out to be similar to my Year 13 friend's; they were making a controller too at the time . But never mind that; I need to figure out how I'll do the joints. What will be the actual dimensions of the project ? How big should it be? How much force does it need to withstand? How much load can the joints handle? I decided on a thickness of 12mm; that seems sturdy but not overly thick. It'll work. But what type of wood should I use? I need it to be strong, so I opted for plywood- high strength and easy to work with. Now, how high and long should I make the project? Will I need to measure the length of a monitor stand ? Okay , 30cm (300mm) seems standard, so I'll make the length 40cm (400mm) . How high? I'll choose 15cm. I'll get those pieces cut and ready, and now it's time to model the joints and test their strength. These joints were able to hold up to 20kg per joint without cracking, only beginning to crack at around 30kg . They were strong and sturdy-perfect! With that, I made the first joint to hold the wood upright. Now I need to create additional joints: one to hold a piece of wood in the middle and another to support a smaller piece. So, I began testing and creating; they were easy to model, but the real challenge was making the mechanical switches fit into my Arduino controller case . Those took a whole lot of testing!

Fusion 360 Designs

While making my final project for my Technology Product Design class in 2024, I also worked on fusion models to show how the design would look once completed. I used these models to 3D print the Arduino controller that I had made. To get exact measurements and make it as best as possible, I had to go into high detail when doing the mechanical keyboard switches for my controller to ensure that they didn't fall out easily and that they could fit in without issues. This took trial and error, but in the end, it had an overwhelmingly good outcome. The space inside was compact and small to where the wires were all bundled up; this was a design flaw, but I couldn't figure out another way to keep it small and compact with good wire management.

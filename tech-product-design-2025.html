First time working with metal- Project chandelier

1/28

This year in my technology class, I was told to make a light for a stakeholder. This light could be anything as long as I made it. With that information, I decided to make a chandelier. Why a chandelier? Well, because of my mom. Ever since I was a little kid, my mom said one day there would be a chandelier hung up there . One day, we would buy one and hang it up. As more years passed, there never was one , and my mom decided to DIY one, which looked alright-not the best, but for what we had, it was amazing. However, she never really felt complete with that, and with age comes fewer abilities and more issues with making things. So when I was told I needed to make a light, the idea sparked instantaneously- my mom. She's the perfect stakeholder. A chandelier will be what I make. What material? Well, iron is expensive and heavy ( I would love to work with it, but not enough funds), so the next best option is aluminum. I can melt it with its low melting point, cast it in sand , and then remove it . What a great idea! I never realized working with molten metal would be scholarship-level difficulty . My teacher at the start never thought I had the abilities to actually make a chandelier. It's too difficult, he said. I asked him to give me a percentage of how likely he thought I would complete the chandelier, and he said 5% . This was the same percentage as last year when I decided to make a monitor stand with a volume mixer Arduino that controls different applications. And so the journey began-to impress my teacher and make my mother proud. The more the year progressed , the more my teacher actually believed in me. Seeing me research and make a furnace to melt the aluminum, learn to weld, and create more precise 3D models of my design to be able to print and cast them made him seem excited. The chandelier that seemed impossible for a Year 12 student, a scholarship/ university- level project, seemed so close to being able to be made. With all the advanced techniques-welding, casting, 3D printing, diffusing light, microcontrollers- but that's all I can say for now, as I'm halfway done and still need to find a 3D printer big enough to print my mold.
